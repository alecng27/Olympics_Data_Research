Olympics Data Research Project

2 Does Wining An Olympic Medal Increase Fertility?

Alec Nguyen

The ancient Olympic Games were hosted in Olympia, Greece between 776 BC and 393 AD. The first modern Olympics were held in Athens, Greece in 1896. All of the Games starting from Athens in 1896 to Beijing 2022 are referred to as the “Modern Olympics”1.

Olympic athletes are always at the forefront to push the human limits. The athletes are a great symbol of pride and inspiration for the countries they represent and compete for. The aim of this project is to explore whether winning at the Olympics influence fertility within those countries.

Specifically, the main question is: Does a country that perform significantly better in the Olympics, meaning they if they obtain medals, experience an observed increase in birthrate?

The findings from this question may reveal what society values. Will there be a national increase in patriotism that leads to more births from a performs well in the Olympics? Do people place value in the performance of their respective countries in the Games that it will influence the when to have children?

This is a very interesting topic to investigate that has relevance to countries with aging population problems such as Japan, Italy, and Germany2. This might give an insight into whether the countries should bid to host future Olympic Games.

Kaggle3 provides comprehensive and detailed datasets containing Olympics-related statistics from 1896 and 2016. Our World In Data4, on the other hand, provides a detailed birth numbers of all countries between 1950 and 2020.
